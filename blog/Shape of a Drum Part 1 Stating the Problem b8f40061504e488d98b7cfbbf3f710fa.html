Shape of a Drum Part 1: Stating the Problem

AuthorJessica Eastman
Date@September 12, 2023

A delve into mathematics

In Mathematics research, one usually poses an interesting question and then spends some time thinking about how to answer that question, and not just answer it but prove it. And that process may take a long time, depending on how hard the question that was posed is.

Fermat’s last theorem was first stated around 1637 and the proof was published in 1995 by Andrew Wiles. But, as with a lot of hard maths problems, the problem itself generated more questions about more problems that were unsolved, leading to the development of an entire field of mathematics known as algebraic number theory. It is a beautiful fact about the study of mathematics that we may pose interesting questions that lead to entire fields of study being developed. It is the questions themselves that stimulate the mind and challenge those who hear it to think for themselves, how could I possibly go about solving this problem? A well posed problem will inevitably lead to some solutions.

Everyone can attempt to solve these questions, but sometimes we do not possess the suitable tools needed to prove the solution. In that case we must either learn the tools necessary or, in a lot of cases, create the tools required for the job. I believe this means that mathematics will continue to grow as we ask more and more impossible questions, branching off ad infinitum and creating new and wonderful directions of thought.

It is a part of mathematics that is not often explained to those just beginning to learn the tools for themselves. Like with the study of music, we regiment it heavily and drill certain already well known theorems into the students of the craft. Because we must know what came previously before we can ourselves create something new. We must develop a firm footing for ourselves before we can confidently proceed. There is no faster way to descend into madness than to tackle problems you are ill-equipped to deal with. But there is no more satisfying endeavour than to solve a problem you have decided to undertake. Mathematics is to me as close as we may come to real magic in the world.

Schooling in mathematics can only work if paired with a passion for pondering interesting mathematical problems in your spare time. But how to learn that passion? It has to come from something you enjoy. Well, I cannot make you enjoy mathematics. That is not the point, and maybe shouldn’t be. The people who find it fascinating will flock to it naturally. For the same reasons that people are naturally drawn to anything else, because there is no choice but to go towards what you are interested in.

That is a round about introduction, all to say really that I am going to discuss some things that I am quite interested in here in a series of blogs, focusing on certain questions posed in mathematics. The journey to solving those questions is in itself fascinating and hopefully we can spend some time detouring to look at the flowers in the fields ourselves.

While I was working as a postdoctoral researcher in London, I found a question that to me was quite interesting. It was well posed in a paper that I stumbled upon after hearing it briefly mentioned in a talk. Naturally I went digging down a rabit hole to find myself absorbed in a mystery of the universe. Weaving its way into my life, I started to wonder at how my work and my place in the universe connected with this question. And it became a thing to appreciate, regardless or not for whether it was particularly relevant. Learning about it brought me joy, and so I share this joy with you all.

The question was posed as this:

Can one hear the shape of a drum?

Now at the time I was deep into research in quantum billiards, for which this question seemed entirely relevant to my work and I got to thinking about how this applies to Non Hermitian quantum billiards. Don’t worry, I will explain a bit about my work shortly. First I must explain what this question is asking somewhat I suppose.

In 1966, Mark Kac posed the rather intriguing question with the title of a paper of his on the topic.

We often think about spectral properties in the reverse, namely given the shape of a drum, what does the spectra look like? Calculating the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions for the given boundary value problem. But can you infer, when given the spectra, what the shape of the drum looks like?

The short answer to the question is no, you cannot tell based on the spectra. But the journey to arriving at that answer is certainly a very interesting one that leads to many other questions along the way. This was not the first time the question had been posed and it would not be the last time, many have shared a similar interest in such a problem, and not just in pure mathematics. I think I first caught wind of it when watching a seminar on Cayley graphs from the London Mathematical Society (LMS) conference on art and mathematics. I feel that it is my duty to note that I didn’t really understand much from the talk about what Cayley graphs even are, but I did find the story of hearing the shape of the drum to be particularly relevant to me at the time since I was working as a postdoc on Quantum Billiards which deal primarily with the spectra of shapes. Later on we will look at how Cayley graphs become relevant to the proof that you cannot hear the shape of a drum. But we haven’t posed the question well yet so we must go back to the beginning.

Schuster 1882

So the question was actually originally posed by Schuster in 1882 who said:

… It would baffle the most skillful mathematician to solve the inverse problem and to find out the shape of a bell by means of the sounds which it is capable of sending out.

Of course I do not know the exact source of this quote since I myself found it in another article on the same question (a secondary source I suppose, or a tertiary source? In any regard, distanced from the original source by quite a bit). I found it quoted in Gordon and Webb’s review from 1996 which does not contain citations, go figure. Apparently Schuster coined the term “Spectroscopy” so we can get a feel for how relevant he was indeed.

Side note: As a physicist and a student with two years of undergraduate chemistry under my belt, I do so enjoy the study of spectroscopy and can’t help but state how great it is here. But we will stick to the relevant mathematical side of things here.

Kac 1966

Now let us look at the paper we are interested in itself, titled “Can one hear the shape of a drum?” and let us pose the question in a somewhat mathematical way. As Kac mentions in the paper itself, he first heard the problem posed ten years prior by Professor Bochner, and when he mentioned the problem to Professor Bers, Bers said:

You mean, if you had perfect pitch could you find the shape of a drum.

How do we pose such a question? Well, in order to hear the shape of a drum we must ask the question, given two shapes and two eigenvalue problems, and say that the two problems give exactly the same eigenvalues, does that mean that the shapes must be the exact same? Ie. Congruent in the sense of Euclidean geometry? Or perhaps we can word it as follows: are there any two differently shaped regions that give the exact same eigenvalues?

Let us distinguish the two plane regions as Ω1\Omega_1Ω1​﻿ and Ω2\Omega_2Ω2​﻿, bounded by the curves Γ1\Gamma_1Γ1​﻿ and Γ2\Gamma_2Γ2​﻿ respectively as shown in the sketch below.

We consider the eigenvalue problem for each:

and we assume for each n, the eigenvalue λn\lambda_nλn​﻿ for Ω1\Omega_1Ω1​﻿ is equal to the eigenvalue μn\mu_nμn​﻿ for Ω2\Omega_2Ω2​﻿. Does this mean the regions must be congruent in the Euclidean sense? Now if this looks unfamiliar to anyone, this is what is known as a Dirichlet Boundary value problem, something you would learn about in a second year undergraduate maths course. We have a particular equation to solve inside of the boundary, knowing that the solutions that a particular value on the boundary itself, namely zero in this case. This problem is solving the Laplace equation specifically, where ∇2\nabla^2∇2﻿ is the Laplace operator. These are exactly the sorts of problems that we tackle when we consider quantum Billiards. So in the next blog we will touch upon Quantum billiards and solving the Laplace equation before moving on with this question, a detour, we will say.

This problem in particular however of whether the two shapes share the same eigenvalues is a question for isospectrality. “Iso-” being originally from the greek “isos”, meaning the same. Saying two problems are isospectral means that we arrive at the exact same spectra for both (eigenvalues). Now there was some previous work that Kac notes in his paper by John Milnor in 1964 that is highly relevant. Milnor constructed two noncongruent 16 dimensional tori (the plural of torus) whose Laplace-Beltrami operators had exactly the same eigenvalues. I suggest looking at the work itself, it is barely the length of a page, making it very succinct (though entirely opaque to most of us). One could guess from this that it would be fair to say there exists two noncongruent drum shapes that share the same eigenvalues. Afterall we are dealing with only two dimensions as opposed to the 16-dimensional case. But proving and guessing are two very different things and it wasn’t until 1992 that Gordon, Webb and Wolpert found two such shapes that are isospectral. And as a side note, I have stumbled down another hole, discovering that in fact it has been harder to show for reduced dimensional tori that they are indeed isospectral. It has been done down to 4 dimensions and Schiemann’s theorem states that it cannot be done for the 3D case:

There is no non-trivial isospectrality between flat tori of dimension 3.

So perhaps guesses will get us nowhere fast and we must never assume anything about the mathematical world we are building around us. Another relevant paper in this context is the paper titled “Hearing the Platycosms” by Rossetti and Conway, which looks like it could be a lot of fun. Apparently Platycosm means flat universe.
